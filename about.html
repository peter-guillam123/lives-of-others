About this project

Lives of Others

An interactive entry point to fifteen years of Guardian obituaries — not as a feed, but as a place you can poke around in.

The Guardian's obit desk publishes around a thousand pieces a year. Most readers will see one or two by accident, on the day somebody famous dies. The rest sit in the archive, doing nothing. They are some of the best writing the paper does and almost nobody reads them.

So this is an attempt to make them browseable, with a few editorial hooks I find more interesting than “latest first”:

  • Who's on today. The front page asks: of the people we have obits for, who was born today, who died today, and whose life ran through an anniversary that lands today?
  • Every life on a timeline. One horizontal line per person, from birth to death. Filter by field; click to read.
  • A quote roulette. The obit writers usually find one line that captures the person. The roulette puts them at the size they deserve.

How it's put together

The site is static HTML — no server, no framework, no analytics. A nightly GitHub Action asks the Guardian's Content API for any new obituaries, sends each one through Claude (Haiku) to extract structured fields (born, died, field, themes, the best pull-quote, names of people mentioned, and any historical anniversaries the life touched), and writes everything back to a few JSON files. Your browser loads about 500 KB of catalogue on first visit; year-shards load on demand.

Source on GitHub. The Claude prompt and the build pipeline are both there if you want to read them.

What it isn't

It isn't a search engine. The obit archive on theguardian.com is, and it's better than anything I could build. It isn't a feed; it isn't a tribute. It's a different way in.

Diary

Reverse-chronological notes on what's been built and why. Each entry stands alone.

Milestone · 21 May 2026

First public version

Front page with three on-this-day lanes, a quote roulette, and a featured strip. Plus a lifespan ribbon — every obit in the archive as a horizontal line from birth to death.

21 May 2026

Adopting Quarto

Switched the design to the Quarto system — a five-token palette and Helvetica + JetBrains Mono pairing inspired by 1960s book cover design. The hard 1px borders and mono-numbered section heads feel right for an obit archive — almanac more than feed.

21 May 2026

The Other-lives leak

The first production run quietly let in around 500–1000 reader-written “Other lives” pieces, mistaking them for full staff obits because the editorial convention has shifted: they're tagged at series level rather than prefixed in the title. Fixed by querying the series tag at fetch time and evicting the bad ones from the existing data. “Other lives” is a different thing — family-voice, often beautiful — and may be its own mode one day.

21 May 2026

Why structured extraction

The Guardian's API gives you the obit text, the byline, the tags, the date of publication. It doesn't give you the year the person was born, or the field they worked in, or the quote that captures them. So the build sends each obit through Claude (Haiku) with a tight schema and a careful prompt. A few pence per nightly run after the back-catalogue is done.

21 May 2026

The shape that didn't get built

The first sketch had a force-directed graph of who was mentioned in whose obituary — pull the strings, find a cluster of physicists, click in. Great on a 27-inch monitor, terrible on a phone. The ribbon is the version that survived the mobile pass. The graph waits for a “Connections” page some other day.
