About

Lives of Others

An interactive entry point to fifteen years of Guardian obituaries — not as a feed, but as a place you can poke around in.

The Guardian's obit desk publishes something like a thousand pieces a year. Most readers will see one or two by accident, on a day when somebody famous dies. The rest sit in the archive, doing nothing. They are some of the best writing the paper does and almost nobody reads them.

So this is an attempt to make them browseable, with a few editorial hooks I find more interesting than “latest first”:

  • Who's on today. The front page asks: of the people we have obituaries for, who was born today, who died today, and whose life ran through an anniversary that lands today? The DNA paper went out on 25 April 1953. There are five people in this archive who were in the room.
  • Every life on a timeline. One horizontal line per person, from birth to death, laid out across a century-and-a-half. You can filter by field. You can also just enjoy the visual fact that most lives overlap with most other lives.
  • A quote roulette. The obit writers usually find one line that captures the person. The roulette pulls those out and shows them at the size they deserve.

How it's put together

The site is static HTML — no server, no framework, no analytics. A nightly GitHub Action does the work: it asks the Guardian's Content API for any new obituaries, sends each one through Claude (Haiku) to extract structured fields (born, died, field, themes, the best pull-quote, names of people mentioned, and any historical anniversaries the life touched), and writes everything back to a few JSON files. Your browser loads about 500 KB of catalogue on first visit; year-shards load on demand.

Source on GitHub. The Claude prompt and the build pipeline are both there if you want to read them.

What it isn't

It isn't a search engine. The obit archive on theguardian.com is, and it's better than anything I could build. It isn't a feed; it isn't a tribute. It's a different way in.

Milestone · 12 May 2026

First public version

Front page with three on-this-day lanes, a quote roulette, and a featured strip. Plus a lifespan ribbon — every obit in the archive as a horizontal line from birth to death. Built in an afternoon with Claude Code.

Two modes only, by design. The connections graph (which obits cite which other obits?) and thematic clusters (cold-war science, the Bloomsbury second-string, the people who were in the room) are diary entries 2 and 3.

12 May 2026

Why structured extraction

The Guardian's API gives you the obit text, the byline, the tags, the date of publication. It doesn't give you the year the person was born, or the field they worked in, or the quote that captures them. To do “on this day in lives” you need that data extracted from the prose, and there's no clean way to do that with regex. So the build sends each obit through Claude (Haiku) with a tight schema and a careful prompt. Costs a few pence per nightly run after the back-catalogue is done.

12 May 2026

The shape that didn't get built

The first sketch had a force-directed graph of who was mentioned in whose obituary — pull the strings, find a cluster of physicists, click into one. It's a great idea on a 27-inch monitor and a terrible idea on a phone. The ribbon is the version that survives the mobile pass. Some day, maybe, on a "graph" page with a confidence warning.
